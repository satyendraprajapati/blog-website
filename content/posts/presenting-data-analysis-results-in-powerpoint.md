A finished Power BI dashboard or Excel model is rarely the end product — most of the time, someone still has to stand up and walk a room through what it means. That's a different skill from the analysis itself, and it's easy to undercut good work with a bad deck.

**1. One slide, one takeaway.** Each slide should answer a single question — "did revenue grow this quarter?" — not host every chart you built along the way. If a slide needs a paragraph to explain what it's showing, split it into two slides.

**2. Put the conclusion in the title, not the chart.** Instead of a slide titled "Regional Revenue," title it "West region drove 60% of Q2 growth." The audience should be able to skim just the titles and still follow your argument.

**3. Screenshot the chart, don't rebuild it.** Paste a static image of your actual Power BI visual or Excel chart rather than recreating a simplified version in PowerPoint's chart tool — it keeps the numbers consistent with the source and saves you from maintaining the chart twice.

**4. Cut every gridline and label the chart isn't using.** Default Excel/Power BI exports carry gridlines, extra axis ticks, and legends that made sense while you were exploring the data but just add noise once you know what the point is.

**5. Put backup detail in an appendix, not the main deck.** Keep the core narrative to 5–8 slides, and move the supporting breakdowns — the ones someone might ask about but most won't — to appendix slides at the end. That keeps the main flow tight without throwing away work you already did.

The analysis convinces you the finding is real; the deck has to convince someone else in five minutes without access to the spreadsheet. Optimize for that.
