A portfolio full of tutorial-following projects — the same Titanic dataset, the same COVID dashboard everyone else built — signals that you can follow instructions, not that you can think like an analyst. Here's what tends to stand out instead.

**1. Pick messy, real-ish data over clean sample datasets.** A dataset that's already tidy and well-documented skips the part of the job that actually takes skill: figuring out what's wrong with the data before you can trust an answer from it. Public datasets from government open-data portals or Kaggle competitions with genuinely inconsistent formatting make for a much more honest showcase.

**2. Lead with the question, not the tool.** "I used Power BI to build a dashboard" tells a hiring manager nothing about your judgment. "I found that 20% of churned customers had a support ticket in their final week — which changed how the team thought about churn triggers" tells them you can turn data into a decision. Frame every project around a question and an answer; mention the tools as supporting detail.

**3. Show your dead ends, briefly.** A short "I initially assumed X, but the data showed Y, so I changed my approach" line does more for your credibility than a polished result with no visible reasoning. It's the clearest signal you actually did the analysis rather than copying a template.

**4. Keep write-ups skimmable.** Most reviewers give a portfolio project 60–90 seconds before deciding whether to read further. Lead with the takeaway in a sentence, follow with the chart, and put your full methodology below the fold — not the other way around.

**5. Have one project you can defend for 20 minutes.** In interviews, you'll usually get asked to walk through one project in depth. Pick your strongest one and be ready to explain every choice — why that chart type, why you handled missing data that way, what you'd do differently with more time. Depth on one project beats breadth across six shallow ones.
